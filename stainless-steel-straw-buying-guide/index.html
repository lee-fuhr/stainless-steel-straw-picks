How to choose stainless steel straws

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A stainless steel straw is a reusable, eco-friendly alternative to single-use plastic. The best choice comes down to size (diameter and length), shape (straight vs. bent), and how you plan to clean it. This guide walks you through what matters so you can pick a set you’ll actually use every day.

Why switch to stainless steel straws?

Plastic straws are notoriously hard to recycle and often end up in oceans. Paper straws get soggy. Stainless steel straws are durable, rust-resistant, and last for years. They don’t leach chemicals, and they feel substantial in your hand, no flimsy bending or wilting. Beyond the environmental angle, stainless steel doesn’t absorb flavors or odors. You can use the same straw for iced coffee, smoothies, and water without any aftertaste. And because they’re smooth and seamless, they’re comfortable on the lips, especially if you choose a polished, rounded tip.

Which diameter and length should you choose?

Straws come in two main diameters: regular (about the size of a typical fast-food straw) and wide (often called smoothie or boba size). A regular-diameter straw works fine for water, soda, or iced tea. For thicker drinks, smoothies, milkshakes, or bubble tea, you’ll want a wide straw so the liquid flows easily without clogging. Length matters too. Most reusable straws are between 8 and 10 inches. A slightly longer straw is better for tall tumblers or travel mugs; a shorter one fits standard glasses. Some sets include both lengths, or you can buy matched pairs for your most-used cups. Measure your go-to glass before ordering to avoid that awkward inch-short reach.

Straight or bent: what’s the difference?

Straight straws are simpler to clean (a single, straight channel) and often come in bulk packs. They look sleek and work well for upright drinking glasses. Bent straws have a gentle angle near the top, which makes it easier to sip from a low-profile cup without tilting your head back. They’re also more comfortable for some people, especially when drinking from a can or a short glass. Bent straws do have a slight curve that can trap liquid if you don’t rinse thoroughly. Many manufacturers now make bent straws with a smooth, seamless bend, check user reviews to see if the bend is clean or has internal ridges. If you drink mostly from tall tumblers, straight straws are fine. If you use cans or short cups, consider a bent set.

How to clean stainless steel straws properly?

Cleaning is the single biggest practical issue with reusable straws. Most sets come with a thin cleaning brush, a long, flexible bristle wand. You should run it through the straw immediately after use, especially if you’ve had a thick drink. Letting residue dry inside can make cleaning harder later. Many stainless steel straws are dishwasher safe, but check the product description. If they’re dishwasher safe, place them vertically in the utensil basket or on the top rack. Some manufacturers recommend occasional boiling or a vinegar soak to remove mineral buildup. A dedicated straw-cleaning brush is worth buying even if your set includes one, as brushes wear out. Look for a brush with a loop on one end so you can hang it to dry.

Are there any safety or comfort concerns?

Quality matters. Cheaper straws may have rough edges or burrs that can cut your lip. Reputable brands polish the ends to a smooth, rounded finish. If you buy an ultra-budget set, run your finger lightly over the rim before the first use. Some people also worry about damaging teeth. Stainless steel is hard, so biting down on a straw could chip a tooth. The fix is simple: don’t bite. Or choose a straw with a silicone tip, which adds a soft buffer. For hot drinks, metal conducts heat. A bare stainless steel straw will get very hot with coffee or tea, plenty hot to burn your mouth. Many brands sell silicone-tipped or completely silicone-coated straws for hot beverages. If you drink hot drinks regularly, look for straws with a silicone sleeve or buy a separate set of silicone straws for that purpose.

How to pick the right set for your lifestyle?

Think about where you’ll use them most. For home use, a large variety pack with multiple diameters and lengths is handy. For travel, a single straw in a carry case, often with a keychain clip, is more practical. Some sets come with a small pouch that fits in a purse or backpack, plus a brush that folds into the case. Consider how many people are in your household. If you entertain often, get at least 8 to 12 straws so you’re not washing between guests. Look for sets that include a matching cleaning brush and maybe even a straw topper (a small silicone cap for the top). Also check the finish: brushed stainless is less slippery, while polished looks shiny but can show fingerprints. Neither affects performance.

Frequently asked questions

Can stainless steel straws be recycled at the end of their life?

Yes, stainless steel is fully recyclable. In practice, the straw is too small to go through most single-stream recycling machinery. To ensure it gets recycled, collect several metal straws and take them to a scrap metal facility, or check if your local recycling center accepts small metal items.

Do stainless steel straws get too hot or too cold to hold?

They conduct temperature, so a straw left in a hot drink will become hot to the touch, and one in a frozen drink can feel icy on your lips. Many people find it manageable for iced drinks, but for hot beverages a silicone tip or a fully silicone straw is much safer.

Are stainless steel straws safe for hot drinks?

The metal itself is heat-safe, but the straw will transfer heat to your mouth. Unless the straw has a silicone mouthpiece, it can burn your lips. If you regularly drink coffee or tea, buy straws with a silicone sleeve or dedicated hot-drink straws.

How many stainless steel straws should I buy for my household?

A good starting point is at least two straws per person, plus a few extras for guests or to keep a spare at the office. A set of 8 to 12 straws is typical for a family. You can always buy an additional pack later if you find yourself running out between dishwasher loads.

Will stainless steel straws scratch my glassware or tumblers?

Stainless steel is harder than glass, so careless stirring or tapping can leave scratches. To avoid that, use a gentle stirring motion and don’t clink the straw against the sides of a delicate glass. Most everyday tumblers and mason jars hold up fine with normal use.

Are stainless steel straws dishwasher safe?

Most are, but you should always confirm with the manufacturer. Dishwasher detergents are generally fine for stainless steel. One caveat: small straws can fall through the dishwasher racks and end up in the heating element. Place them vertically in a utensil basket or in the top rack to be safe.

In shortBuying stainless steel straws is a small change that can visibly cut down your single-use plastic waste. Focus on getting a diameter that matches the drinks you pour most often, choose a shape that works with your favorite cups, and never skip the cleaning brush. A well-chosen set will last for years without rust, stains, or bending, and you’ll wonder why you didn’t switch sooner.
